Secure processing

Your files are processed by your browser on your local machine using JavaScript, webR, DuckDB, and Rduckhts. No interval data is transferred away from your local machine.

BED validation

Your file is validated against BEDv1 file specifications. File delimiter, newline character, 0-length intervals, and other common issues are reported.

Try an example

We have prepared two examples to discover DuckBedQC features.

Analyzing your BED file

Check interval density by chromosome

You can look at the interval density or count by chromosome compared to what would be expected based on the relative length of each chromosome. For example, segmental duplications have a much higher density than expected on chr16 in the human genome.

Find truncated files

Use interval counts plot to diagnose truncated or incomplete files based on the expected chromosomes of a genome build.

More complex analyses

For instance, you can compare your intervals against bundled centromere, gene, exon, and CDS annotations from multiple builds to sanity-check genome build assumptions and overall BED content.

Interpreting annotation coverage
Intersecting a BED with known annotations can help narrow down likely genome builds and confirm that the intervals match the kind of regions you expect. In this fork, DuckBedQC ships bundled centromere, whole-gene locus, exon, and CDS tables that are queried through DuckDB in the browser. The coverage view reports annotation interval hit rate, annotation base coverage, and exports covered or uncovered annotation BED rows for kit-style review.

Normal

If intervals show little overlap with centromeres for the selected build, that build remains a plausible explanation for the uploaded BED.

Potential issue

If intervals overlap many centromere intervals for the selected build, the BED may belong to a different build or include unexpected regions.
